Hallucinations in Large Language Models

Hallucination in the context of large language models refers to the phenomenon where a model generates text that is fluent and confident-sounding but factually incorrect, fabricated, or unsupported by the input context. This is one of the most significant challenges facing the deployment of language models in high-stakes applications such as healthcare, legal analysis, and financial reporting. Despite their impressive generative capabilities, language models do not possess genuine understanding or access to a verified knowledge base, making them prone to producing plausible but unfounded claims.

Hallucinations can be broadly categorized into two types. Intrinsic hallucinations occur when the generated output contradicts the information provided in the input or source material. For example, a summarization model might attribute a statement to the wrong person mentioned in the original document. Extrinsic hallucinations involve the model generating information that cannot be verified from the input context at all, such as fabricating citations, inventing statistics, or creating fictitious entities. Both types are problematic, but extrinsic hallucinations are particularly dangerous because they can introduce entirely false information that appears authoritative.

Several factors contribute to hallucinations. During pre-training, models learn statistical patterns from massive datasets that may contain errors, contradictions, or biased information. The model's objective of predicting the next most likely token prioritizes fluency over factual accuracy, incentivizing the generation of plausible-sounding text regardless of its truthfulness. Additionally, when a model encounters queries outside the scope of its training data, it tends to extrapolate rather than acknowledge uncertainty.

Multiple strategies have been proposed to mitigate hallucinations. Retrieval-Augmented Generation grounds the model's responses in retrieved evidence from external knowledge sources. Fine-tuning with human feedback teaches the model to produce more accurate and calibrated outputs. Other approaches include constrained decoding, which limits the model's generation to verified facts, and chain-of-thought prompting, which encourages step-by-step reasoning. Despite these efforts, completely eliminating hallucinations remains an open research problem, and human oversight continues to be essential in critical applications.
