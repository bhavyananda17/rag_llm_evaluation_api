Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Retrieval-Augmented Generation, commonly referred to as RAG, is a hybrid approach that combines the strengths of information retrieval systems with the generative capabilities of large language models. The core idea behind RAG is straightforward yet powerful: instead of relying solely on the knowledge encoded in a model's parameters during pre-training, the system retrieves relevant documents or passages from an external knowledge base at inference time and uses them as additional context for generating responses.

The RAG pipeline typically consists of three main components. First, a retriever searches a document corpus to identify the most relevant passages given a user's query. This retriever often uses dense vector representations, where both queries and documents are embedded into a shared vector space using models like sentence transformers, and similarity is computed using metrics such as cosine similarity or dot product. Second, the retrieved passages are concatenated with the original query to form an augmented prompt. Third, a generator, usually a pre-trained language model, processes this augmented input and produces a coherent response grounded in the retrieved information.

One of the primary advantages of RAG is its ability to provide up-to-date and domain-specific information without requiring the language model to be retrained. Since the external knowledge base can be updated independently, the system can incorporate new information in real time. This makes RAG particularly valuable in applications such as customer support, legal research, medical question answering, and enterprise knowledge management, where accuracy and recency of information are critical.

RAG also helps mitigate a well-known limitation of large language models: hallucination. By grounding the model's responses in retrieved evidence, the likelihood of generating fabricated or factually incorrect information is significantly reduced. However, the quality of the system's output depends heavily on the retriever's ability to identify truly relevant passages. Poor retrieval can lead to irrelevant context being injected, which may confuse the generator and degrade response quality. Advanced RAG systems address this through techniques like re-ranking, query expansion, and iterative retrieval to improve the relevance and coverage of retrieved documents.
